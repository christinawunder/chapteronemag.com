Why my first boss hired me:

About half a year after I had started my very first real job, this is now 4 years ago, I had a very interesting conversation with my boss at the time. In a personal feedback discussion he explained to me why he had decided to hire me, instead of any of the hundreds of other applicants.

I could make this an article about "how to write a good application letter", but I won't. I want this article to be about something else.

Let's assume that I followed the job applications etiquette manual. I tailored the letter to the company, I more or less understood from the job description what they were looking for, and I presented my skills and competencies in an easy-to-digest manner. And still:

My application had been discarded in the pre-selection. Sorry, not sorry.

Because the competing applicants had many years of working experience, and I barely managed to count one and a half (being generous that is, and adding all my internships together).

Luckily, the boss had decided to look through the "No"-pile, where he found my application. He decided to put my application (and a couple of others) back on the "Maybe"-pile. I was back in the race.

He explained to his colleague: "Listen, we're trying to fill a post that has limited contract for one year. You have to consider that nobody, who already has a full-fledged career, will be willing to quit their job and jump into uncertainty, for only one year. That wouldn't be fair."

That is why he decided to give a young person a chance. This reasoning was noble and pragmatic at the same time: of course you could give this job to somebody, who already has lots of experience, but for whom a one-year contract would be a leap into uncertainty, and essentially a step down. This person is likely to become dissatisfied with what we have to offer - and that would be a bad thing for them, as well as for us.

"If, however, we hire someone who is rather unexperienced and young, but all the more motivated and dynamic, it's a win-win-situation for both: we give this person a chance to kick-start their career. For this person, a one-year contract so shortly after graduating university is a jackpot. And a person who is happy and motivated, is very likely to end up doing good work. It's very simple."

I believe that every employer should stop and think about this for a second. Longstanding experience is important, agreed. But it's not everything. What many forget is that us young people, we are very capable, you know.

We are educated, curious, motivated and willing to learn.

All we need is a chance.

My then boss took a risk in hiring me, because not everyone agreed with his approach. "Too junior, too inexperienced, not fit for the job", were the common worries.

But he proved them wrong. I proved them wrong.
